Building High-Performing Engineering Teams: Lessons from Scaling 5x

Scaling an engineering team from 20 to 100 people taught me that team building isn't just about hiring‚Äîit's about creating systems, culture, and practices that enable people to do their best work. Here's what I learned about building teams that scale.

The Scaling Challenge

When we started scaling, I assumed that what worked for 20 people would work for 100. I was wrong. Small team practices don't scale linearly. What worked at 20 people broke down at 50, and what worked at 50 needed reinvention at 100.

Key challenges:

  • Maintaining code quality and consistency across teams
  • Preserving culture and values as team grows
  • Enabling autonomy while maintaining alignment
  • Hiring fast without compromising quality
  • Building effective communication across teams

Hiring: Quality Over Speed

The biggest mistake I see is prioritizing speed over quality in hiring. A bad hire costs more than a delayed hire. We developed a rigorous but efficient hiring process that maintained quality while scaling.

Our hiring framework:

  • Structured interviews: Consistent evaluation criteria across all candidates
  • Technical assessments: Real-world problems, not algorithm puzzles
  • Culture fit evaluation: Assess alignment with team values
  • Panel interviews: Multiple perspectives reduce bias
  • Fast feedback loops: Decision within 48 hours

Onboarding: Setting People Up for Success

Great onboarding is the difference between someone contributing in weeks versus months. We built a comprehensive onboarding program that accelerated time-to-productivity.

Onboarding components:

  • Structured first-week program with clear milestones
  • Assigned mentor for first 90 days
  • Documentation and runbooks for common tasks
  • Small, meaningful first project within first week
  • Regular check-ins and feedback sessions

Team Structure: From Flat to Hierarchical

At 20 people, a flat structure works. At 100, you need hierarchy. But hierarchy doesn't mean bureaucracy. We designed a structure that maintained autonomy while enabling coordination.

Our structure:

  • Squads: 5-8 person teams with clear ownership
  • Tribes: Groups of related squads (15-30 people)
  • Engineering Managers: People management and team health
  • Tech Leads: Technical direction and architecture
  • Product Owners: Business alignment and prioritization

Communication: Scaling Information Flow

As teams grow, communication becomes the bottleneck. We implemented multiple communication channels and practices to ensure information flowed effectively.

Communication practices:

  • Weekly all-hands with transparent updates
  • Architecture decision records (ADRs) for major decisions
  • Tech talks and knowledge sharing sessions
  • Cross-team collaboration channels
  • Regular retrospectives and feedback loops

Career Development: Growing People

High-performing teams require growth opportunities. We built career frameworks and development programs that helped people advance while contributing to team success.

Development initiatives:

  • Clear career ladders for IC and management tracks
  • Regular performance reviews with growth plans
  • Learning budgets and conference attendance
  • Internal mentorship programs
  • Stretch assignments and cross-team projects

Key Takeaways

  • Hire slowly, fire quickly. Quality hiring prevents problems down the line.
  • Invest in onboarding. Good onboarding accelerates productivity and improves retention.
  • Structure enables scale. Hierarchy isn't bad; bad hierarchy is bad.
  • Communication is infrastructure. Invest in communication practices as you scale.
  • Grow people, not just teams. Career development is retention strategy.

Building high-performing teams at scale requires intentional design. It's not about finding perfect people‚Äîit's about creating systems and culture that enable good people to do great work together. The teams that scale successfully are those that invest in hiring, onboarding, structure, communication, and development from day one.
